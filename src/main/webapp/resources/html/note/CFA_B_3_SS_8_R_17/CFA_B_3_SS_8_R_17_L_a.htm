A) ASSET ALLOCATION

 

1.     Strategic Asset Allocation:

a.       Combines capital market expectations and the investor’s IPS.

b.       It is called “policy portfolio”

c.       Specifies the investor’s desired exposures to systematic risk

 

2.     Tactical asset allocation:

a.       Make short term adjustments to asset class weights to capitalize on mispricing

 

3.     Asset Only and ALM approach

a.       ALM: involves explicitly modeling liabilities and fund liabilities.

b.       AO approach: does NOT involve explicitly modeling liabilities and fund liabilities.

 

4.     ALM – dynamic approach vs static approach:

 

·         Dynamic approach: link to its investment decision available at all future time periods

·         Static approach: does not consider links to its investment decision available at all future time periods

·         ALM is favored when –

a.       The penalty of not meeting the liabilities is high.

b.       The investor has below average risk tolerance.

c.       The market value of liabilities are interest sensitive.

d.       Legal/tax incentives favor holding fixed-income securities.

·         Investor’s expected utility

 

Um = E(Rm) – 0.005 RAσ2p

Um = Investor's expected utility from asset mix.

E(Rm) = Expected return of asset mix.

RA = Investor's risk aversion.

σ2p  = Variance of return of asset mix.

 

6.     Roy’s first safety criterion

Like sharp ration, it replaces risk free rate with minimum acceptable return. Minimum accepted return (short fall return) = return should not fall below the level to achieve the financial objective.

 

7.     Criteria for specifying asset classes

a.       Assets within an asset class should be relatively homogeneous.

b.       Asset classes are mutually exclusive

c.       Asset classes are diversifying with low correlation

d.       The classes include most of the world’s investable assets.

 

8.     Behavioral bias in asset allocation

 

1.       Loss Aversion.

2.       Mental accounting,

3.       Regret (feat of regret).

 

 

9.     TIPS (Treasury Inflation protected Securities) as an assets class-

 

1.       TIPS of varying maturities are strongly correlated to each other.

2.       Correlation with traditional, nominal bonds and equities are low.

3.       Respond to a different economic variable (real interest rate).

4.       Provide inflation protection (as opposed to regular bonds).

 

10.Criteria of adding new asset class to the portfolio

 

If the Sharpe ratio of new portfolio (after addition) is greater than the sharpe ratio of the existing portfolio after considering the correlation between existing asset's return and the new asset return then we would want to add the new asset.  If the correlation is low then then diversification benefits of adding the new assets to the portfolio will be greater.
