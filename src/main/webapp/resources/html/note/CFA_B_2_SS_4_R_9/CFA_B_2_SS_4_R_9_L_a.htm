A) TAXES

 

1.     Tax Regimes

 

A)  Flat System

Flat tax is paid regardless the income level.

 

B) Progressive System

Tax rate increases as income level increases.

 

                                      i.            Common Progressive

It's a most common regime globally. Many countries provide favorable treatment to investment income like (Interest, dividend or capital gain).

                                     ii.            Light Capital Gain

Another most popular regime where favorable tax treatment is given to capital gain income.

 

2.     Effect of taxes on wealth accumulation

 

 

A)  Accrual Tax

 

Generally charged periodically (annually). Interest income is generally taxed on accrual basis.

 

Formula: FVIF = [1 + r(1 – Tinterest)]n

 

FVIF = Future Value Interest Factor: Future value of initial $1 invested at the end of investment period.

r = Return before considering taxes

n = number of year

Tinterest = Tax rate on interest income.

 

 

Longer Time Horizon(n>1) : Large tax drag because less income will be invested in next period because of taxes paid in the current period.

Higher Rate of return(r): Large tax drag due to compounding (effective tax rate (tax drag) > tax rate).

Higher Time Horizon(n) and Higher Level of return(r):  Much Large tax drag due to above mentioned two points.

 

 

 

B) Deferred Capital Gains

Tax recognition is deferred until the asset is sold. By differing the taxes, Incomes is compounded over time before paid out as taxes.

 

Formula:

FVIF = (1 + r)n – [(1 + r)n – 1]Tcg 

FVIF = (1 + r)n Tcg– (1 + r)n Tcg+ Tcg 

FVIF =(1 + r)n(1 – Tcg) + Tcg

 

Tcg  =Tax rate on capital gain

 

Longer Time Horizon(n>1) : Tax drag amount increases with the time horizon as amount of income increases.

Higher Rate of return(r): Tax drag due to higher rate return but it’s not because of compounding. Taxes are paid at the end of the period (effective tax rate = tax rate)

Higher Time Horizon(n) and Higher Level of return(r):  Tax drag will be present.

 

 

Cost Basis- Above equation assumes cost basis of the asset is equal to 1.  If a security has risen in value since its initial purchase, the cost basis may be less than its current market value. An investment with low cost basis needs an adjustment in the equation.

 

FVIF =(1 + r)n(1 – Tcg) +BTcg

 

B = Adjustment to the cost basis. Usually B<=1.

 

 

C) Wealth/Property Tax

It is restricted to real estate properties.  It applies to both the principal and income components.

 

Formula:

FVIF = [(1 + r)(1 – Tw)]n

 

Tw =Tax rate on Wealth and Property

 

Longer Time Horizon(n>1): Large tax drag.

Higher Rate of return(r): Less Tax drag PERCENTAGE as major portion of the taxes are on the principal.

Higher Time Horizon(n) and Higher Level of return(r):  Time Horizon increases the tax drag percentage and rate decreases the tax drag percentage so net effect

is neutral.

 

2.     Blended Taxation

 

So far, we have seen tax impact on an individual component of income. If we see, impact of taxes in totality (across various components of returns), then individual's realized tax return will be weighted average of tax return of individual components.

 

Weighted average of tax rate T*= PITI + PDTD + PCGTCG

 

(realized after tax return) R* = R(1 – PITI - PDTD  - PCGTCG)

 

P= Portion of Income

D= Dividend

I= interest

CG= Realized capital gain.

 

Unrealized capital gain portion of the investment return has avoided the annual taxation, and tax on that portion would then be deferred until the end of the investment horizon. We are not explaining those calculation because of complexity and not important from the exam perspective.

 

3.     Tax Location

 

 

 

 

A) Tax-Deferred Accounts(TDA)

Contribution is Tax Exempt; Taxes are paid on both principal and the return at the end. IRA is an example.

 

Formula:

FVIF= (1 + r) n (1 – Tn)

 

Tn =Tax rate at the end.

 

B) Tax Exempt Account

 

Contribution is after Tax; Both principal (already taxed) and the returns are not taxable at the end.

 

FVIF = (1 + r) n 

 

In deciding between these two accounts (TDA vs TEA), comparison should be made between current's tax rate and expected future tax rate. If expected future tax will be high then invest in tax exempt account otherwise invest in tax deferred account.

 

Generally, we have regulatory limits on dollar contribution allowed to each account so in this case, Tax exempt account will provide superior after tax return provided we have same tax rate because more principal can be invested on after tax basis, which will compound at risk free rate.

 

 

4.     Accrual Equivalent Returns

 

It is the tax-free return that produces the same after-tax return as the portfolio (provided returns are accrued annually).

 

Example:

Calculate the ending value after taxes at the end of 12 years.

Accrual equivalent annual return % = 100% x [(Ending value after tax/Beginning value)1/12 - 1]

 

Accrued equivalent after tax rate.

 

R (1 – TAE) = RAE

TAE = RAE   - 1

 R

TAE = Realized tax drag.  It is the tax rate that makes the pre-tax return equal to the accrual equivalent return.

 

 

5.     Taxes and Investment Risk

By taxing investment returns, a government shares risk return with the investor. Standard deviation of after-tax returns for a taxable account is σ (1 − tax rate). The risk the investor is actually taking on is now(1 − tax rate) of the pretax risk. 

 

6.     Tax Loss Harvesting

Realized losses from one investment is used to offset realized gains from other investments in the given year.  Realizing a loss saves taxes in the current year, and this tax savings can be reinvested. To gain time value of money(TVM) gains, generally investors sell investments in losses on fourth quarter and sell investments in profits in the first quarter to defer tax payments until next year.

 

7.     Highest-in, first-out (HIFO)

Investors sells the higher cost basis investment first, which defers the tax liability in the following years for lower cost basis investments. If tax rates are projected to increase, it may be beneficial to sell lower-cost basis investments first.
