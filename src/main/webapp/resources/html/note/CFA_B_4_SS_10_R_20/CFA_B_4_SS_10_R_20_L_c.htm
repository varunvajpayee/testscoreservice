C) IMMUNIZATION

 

 

1.     Interest Rate Risk

 

 

 

 

 

When interest rates go up=> Price of the bond go down but Reinvestment income from coupon are high.

When interest rates go down=> Price of the bond go up but Reinvestment income from coupon are low.

 

2.     Classical Immunization

 

Bond portfolio is constructed in such way that change in value of the portfolio is counterbalanced by the return from the reinvestment of the coupon and principal payments. To accomplish this, effective duration of liability should match with portfolio's effective duration (Investor's asset). Classical immunization theory only considers one time parallel changes in yield curve.

 

For example: -

When interest rates go up=> loss from bond value decline approximately equal to Income from Reinvestment.

When interest rates go down=> Gain from bond value incase approximately equal to loss from Reinvestment.

 

That is, immunization requires offsetting price risk and reinvestment risk. In other words, creation of a fixed-income portfolio that produces an assured return for a specific time horizon, irrespective of any parallel shifts in the yield curve.

 

2(a) Immunizing a Single Liability

 

To immunize a single liability effectively:

 

1.       Bond's effective duration = Effective duration of the liability.

2.       Present Value of Bond = Present Value of the liability.

 

If duration of the bond is not equal to duration of liability, then

 

If Duration of bond < Liability duration you have reinvestment risk. (Losses from reinvested income will not be covered by gains of bond's value)

If Duration of bond > Liability duration you have price risk. (gains from reinvested income will not fully offset gains of bond's value)

 

Rebalancing of portfolio

Classical immunization theory only considers one time parallel changes in yield curve but interest rate changes quite frequently and duration of both portfolio and liability changes as time passes. so to keep a portfolio immunized, it must be rebalanced periodically.

 

Immunizing against Nonparallel shifts

Since parallel shifts in interest rate are rarely happen so to keep the portfolio immunized, bond manager should choose a portfolio which minimizes its reinvestment risk.

 

1.       Zero Coupon bond portfolio whose maturity coincide with liability due period, that portfolio will have zero reinvestment risk so it's preferable over other portfolios.

2.       If cash flows from portfolios are fall around the liability date, then reinvestment risk will be low. If the dispersion of cash flow (Maturity Variance M2) is greater around the liability date then reinvestment risk will be higher. So, portfolio with low Maturity variances are preferred.

 

 

3.     Extensions to Classical Immunization

 

3(a) Multifunctional duration (Key Rate Duration)

Manager focuses on key interest rates duration.

 

3(b) Relaxation of minimum risk requirement (Return Maximization)

If manager meets the liability requirements, he can take the greater amount of risk to maximize the return.

 

3(c) Multiple liabilities Immunization

Additional conditions need to be met to support multiple liability immunization against parallel shift in the interest rate.

 

                                       i.            Present Value of asset and liability should be the same.

                                     ii.            Duration of asset and liability should be the same.

                                   iii.            Range of the distribution of durations of the assets must be greater than the distribution of the liabilities.

 

3(d) Contingent Immunization

It is a combined approach of active and passive management of the portfolio to maximize the return and minimize the risk. It provides a flexibility in pursuing active strategies while ensuring a certain minimum return in the case of a parallel rate shift. If portfolio cannot meet this minimum rate, then portfolio manager fall back to the immunization strategy (passive).

 

Minimum acceptable return = Cushion spread + contingent immunization safety net rate

 

Example Questions:

 

20 million portfolio needs to be invested in a bond portfolio for four years. Minimum acceptable rate of return is 6%(Bond equivalent yield) and contingent immunization safety net rate is 4%(Bond equivalent yield).

Answer:

 

Cushion Spread = Minimum acceptable return - contingent immunization safety net rate (6-4=2%%)

Minimum required terminal value (at the end of four years) to pursue contingent immunization safety net rate is: -

20,000,000 * (1.04/2)8 = $23,433,187

 

Required assets at the beginning of-four-year-investment time horizon to grow to immunized terminal value at end-of four-year investment time horizon,

= 23,433,187/ (1.06/2)8 = $18,498,374

 

Initial dollar safety margin is = 20,000,000 - $18,498,374 = $ 1,501,625

 

4.     Immunization Risks

 

Three sources of this risk are interest rate risk, contingent claim risk, and cap risk which can impact Immunization

 

Interest rate risk.

Because the prices of most fixed-income securities move opposite to interest rates, a rising interest rate environment will adversely the value of a portfolio.

 

Contingent claims risk

Call risk and prepayment risk

 

Cap risk.

An asset that makes floating rate payments will typically have caps associated with the floating rate. The manager is at risk of the level of market rates rising while the asset returns are capped.

 

 

5.     Cash flow matching (Dedicated strategy)

Construct a portfolio whose cash flows match the amount and timing of the liabilities (Indirectly causes duration of liability to be matched as well). This is done by working backwards from the last liability date. Since the duration will stay matched as time passes so theoretically rebalancing should not be needed. Cash flow strategy is more conservative and stringent way to meet the liabilities.

 

Classical immunization requires less capital to fund liabilities. (or Why cash flow match strategy is more expensive?)

This is because

 

a) Cash flow matching strategy usually needs to hold cash to meet the liabilities while an immunized portfolio is essentially fully invested at the remaining horizon duration; so, cash drag makes the cash flow matching strategy costlier.

 

b) Funds from a cash flow-matched portfolio must be available on or before each liability due date, which tends to reduce the assumed rate of return. (another example of cash drag)

 

6.     Horizon matching (combination strategy)

Cash flow matching strategy is followed first few years and followed by duration matching strategy in later years. It proves cheaper than cash flow matching strategy but expensive than duration matching strategy.
