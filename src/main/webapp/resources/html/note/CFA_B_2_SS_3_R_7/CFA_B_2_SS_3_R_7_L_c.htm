OTHER POINTS

 

 

1.     Behavioral Finance and Analyst Forecasts (SHIA-O)

 

 

1.       Self-attribution bias

2.       Hindsight bias-“I knew it all along”

3.       Illusion of control

4.       Availability bias

5.       Overconfidence

 

 

Remedial Actions for Overconfidence and Related Biases

 

1.       prompt and accurate feedback to analyst about his/her biases in forecast.

2.       Well-structured feedback can also reduce hindsight bias.

3.       Analyst should use Bayesian approach to update his/her forecast based on new information.

 

 

2.     Analyst Biases in Conducting Research (congam)

 

1.       Confirmation bias.

2.       The gamblers’ fallacy, a misunderstanding of probabilities in which people wrongly project reversal to a long-term mean, is a related cognitive bias. Example- expectation of tail after consecutive 5 heads (law of large number only works when your item set is large).

 

3.     Presentation of Information by Company Management

 

1.       Framing: Managers presents the good Information first.

2.       Anchoring and adjustment: - Anchored to their previous forecast and fail to adjust based on new information.

3.       Availability: - Ease with which information is obtained, recalled, and presented.

 

The best way to deal with management biases regarding the interpretation of information is by maintaining a disciplined and systematic research approach. Focusing on metrics and comparable data, rather than what is descriptive or unverifiable, can assist forecast accuracy and consistency of approach across research.

 

4.     Investment Committees

 

Social proof is a bias in which people follow the beliefs of a group. Analysts may wrongly favor the judgment or endorsement of others, often without being fully aware about it. Groupthink has traditionally led to poor decisions by investment committees because feedback is inaccurate and slow and systematic biases are left unidentified.

For example, a sell-side analyst’s investment view might be influenced by his or her team’s short positions in securities.

 

Remedial Actions for Social proof bias

 

 

·         Create a committee of individuals with a diverse background

·         Ensure members are not afraid to express their opinion (have committee chair ensure this)

·         Actively solicit opinions from each member

 

 

5.     Behavioral Finance and Market Behavior

 

Herding (Momentum) occurs when large number of investors trade on the same side of the market in the same securities, or when investors ignore their own knowledge and information and act as other investors do.

 

Market bubbles: It is characterized by fast increase in the price of securities due to heavy buying.

 

Market Crashes: It is characterized by fast fall in the price of securities due to heavy selling.

 

Home bias: occurs when investor favors investing in their domestic country not in foreign countries securities.
