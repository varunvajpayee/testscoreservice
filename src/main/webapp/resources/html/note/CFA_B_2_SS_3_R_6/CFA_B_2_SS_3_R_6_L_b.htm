I. Belief Perseverance biases

 

1. Conservatism Bias

 

A belief perseverance bias in which people maintain their prior views or forecasts by

inadequately incorporating new information.

The new information that is abstract and statistical is cognitively costly, it thus receives less

weighting.

Fail to modify their beliefs and actions to the extent rationally justified by the new information

and continue to maintain beliefs close to those based on previous estimates and information.

In Bayesian terms, they tend to overweight the base rate (i.e. the probability of the event

without the new information) and underweight the new information.

People tend to cling tenaciously to a view or a forecast. Once a position has been stated, most

people find it very hard to move away from that view. When movement does occur, it does so

only very slowly.

Examples)

Analysts are exceptionally good at telling you what has just happened. They have

invested too heavily in their view, and hence will only change it when presented with

indisputable evidence of its falsehood.

2. Confirmation Bias

A belief perseverance bias in which people tend to look for and notice what confirms their

beliefs, and to ignore or undervalue what conflicts their beliefs (i.e. a process of selective

exposure, selective perception, and selective retention)

Results: Under-diversified portfolio, leading to excessive exposure to risk.

3. Representativeness bias

A belief perseverance bias in which people tend to classify new information based on past

experiences and classifications. They believe their classifications are appropriate and place

undue weight on them.

1) Base rate neglect (Relying on Stereotype)

In base-rate neglect, probability of the categorization is not adequately considered. People rely on stereotypes when making investment decisions without adequately incorporating the base

probability of the stereotype occurring.

2) Sample size neglect (law of small numbers)

In sample-size neglect, people incorrectly assume that sample sizes are representative of

populations. They overweight the information in the small sample.

Examples)

When people do not initially comprehend a trend or pattern reflected in a series of data, they may make assumptions relying on only a few data points.

Result:  High turnover of investment managers

4. Illusion of control bias

Expectancy of a personal success probability inappropriately higher than the objective probability would warrant. People tend to believe that they can control or influence outcomes when, in fact, they can’t.

Emotional Bias – sibling concepts

 Illusion of knowledge (belief you know thing you do not know)

 Self-attribution (belief you personally caused something to happen)

Overconfidence (an unwarranted belief you are correct)

Results:

Excessive Trading

Failure to adequate diversification

Concentrated position in the companies they work for.

5. Hindsight Bias (Selective memory)

When people look back, they do not have perfect memory; they tend to “fill in the gaps” with

what they prefer to believe. People tend to remember their correct views and forgot the errors. In doing so, people may prevent themselves from learning from the past.

 

Results:

Overconfidence

Unfair assessment of money managers.
