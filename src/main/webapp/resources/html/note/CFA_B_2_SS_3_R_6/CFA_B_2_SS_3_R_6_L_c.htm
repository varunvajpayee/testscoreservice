II. Information-Processing Biases

 

1. Anchoring and adjustment bias.

When required to estimate a value with unknown magnitude, people generally begin by

envisioning some initial default number (an anchor) which they then adjust up or down to reflect

subsequent information and analysis. People anchor and adjust because they are generally better

at estimating relative comparison than absolute figures.

 

Conservatism Vs. Anchoring and adjustment bias.

Conservatism bias: People overweight past information compared to new information.

Anchoring and adjustment bias: People place undue weight on the anchor.

Conservatism –anchor is original estimate (past information).

 

2. Mental accounting bias

One sum of money is treated differently (non-fungible or non-interchangeable) from another

equal-sized sum depending how it is categorized. Mental accounts are based on arbitrary

classifications as the source of money (e.g., salary, bonus, inheritance, gambling) or the planned

use of the money (e.g., leisure, necessities)

A potentially serious problem that mental accounting create is the placement of investments into

discrete “buckets” without regard for the correlations among these assets, leading to portfolio

construction in a layered pyramid format)

Behavioral portfolio theory is a goal-based theory, in which each layer of the portfolio

addresses a particular investment goal (e.g., money for retirement) that is independent of other

investment goals (e.g., college fund).

 

3. Framing bias.

The answer given is affected by the way in which the question is asked or “framed”. How

information is processed is dependent upon how the question is framed.

Narrow framing occurs when people evaluate the information to make a decision based on a

narrow frame of reference. People lose sight of big picture and focus on one or two specific points.

 

Results:

   Overly risk-averse or risk-seeking

    Suboptimal Portfolio

    Frequent trading

 

4. Availability Bias (Mental Shortcut)

People tend to put undue emphasis(probability) on the information that is readily available.

 

4 causes of availability bias

1) Retrievability: focus on what is first thought of.

2) Categorization

3) Narrow range of experience: →Frame of reference is too narrow

4) Resonance: People tend to assume what interests them is representative of what other people will find important

 

Result:

Inappropriate asset allocation
