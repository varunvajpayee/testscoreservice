Emotional Biases (LOSERS)

 

1.     Loss-Aversion Bias

 

People tend to strongly prefer avoiding losses (risk-seeking behavior in loss frame) than

to achieve gains (risk-avoiding behavior in gain frame) When comparing absolute values,

the utility derived from a gain is much lower than the utility given up with an equivalent

loss.

 

Results:

Disposition effect: Holding losers too long even if an investment has little or no chance

of going back up, and selling winners too quickly.

Risk avoidance when evaluating a potential gain, thus limiting upside profits → Excessive

trading.

Holding riskier than optimal portfolio.

 

 Myopic Loss Aversion = Time-horizon based framing + mental accounting + loss aversion bias.

Investors evaluate their portfolios on an annual basis and thus overemphasize

short-term gains and losses and weigh losses heavily than gains. The emphasis on short-term

losses result in a higher than theoretically justified equity risk premium.

 

 

2.     Overconfidence Bias

People demonstrate unwarranted faith in their own intuitive reasoning, judgments, and cognitive

abilities.

 

Two causes:

Illusion of knowledge bias: People believe that they are smarter and more informed

than they are.

Self-attribution bias: People take credit for success and assign responsibility for failure.

Success is attributed to the individual’s skill, while failures are attributed to external

factors.

 

3.     Self-control bias

 Hyperbolic discounting: the human tendency to prefer small payoffs now compared to larger

payoffs in the future. Sacrifices in the present require much greater payoffs in the future;

otherwise, people will not be willing to make current sacrifices. People seem to have temporal

short-sightedness or temporal myopia, focusing on the present and discounting the future.

 

4. Status Quo bias (Do nothing instead of making a change)

 

People are generally more comfortable keeping things the same than with change and thus do

not necessarily look for opportunities where change is beneficial. Given no apparent problem

requiring decision, the status quo is maintained

 

5. Endowment bias (discrepancy between “willing to pay” and “willing to buy”)

Ownership “endows” the asset with additional value (emotional attachment). Endowment bias may

apply to inherited or purchased securities.

 

6. Regret-aversion Bias (tendency to stay in low-risk investment)

People tend to avoid making decisions that will result in action out of fear that the decision will

turn out poorly. Regret is more intense when the unfavorable outcomes are the results of an error

of commission versus an error of omission. Thus, no action becomes the preferred decision.
