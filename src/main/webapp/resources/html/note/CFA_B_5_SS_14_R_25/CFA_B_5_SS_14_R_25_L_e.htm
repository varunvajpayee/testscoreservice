E) CREDIT RISK

 

Risk of counterparty defaulting (partially paying) his/her obligation. Credit risk is always one-sided.

CE- Current Exposure - Based current cash flows.

PE- Potential Exposure - Exposure includes all future cash flows.

 

Other credit risk terms: -

 

a.       LGD (Loss Given Default)

b.       PD (Probability of default)

c.       RR (Recovery Rate)

 

1.     Credit VAR (C-VAR)

 

Like VaR, it’s a right tail risk measure because credit risk is higher when expected incoming cash flows are highest.

Example: a company CVaR over one month to be $10 million with a confidence interval of 95%. This would mean there is a (5%) chance of a loss larger than $10 million in the next one month.

 

Difficulty in interpreting Credit VaR

 

1.       Credit VaR is a right tail risk whereas Market VaR is a left tail risk.

2.       Difficulty in calculating PD, RR and LGD.

3.       Exposure is difficult to calculate because considering correlation, aggregation groups, Collateral agreements, and netting agreements.

4.       Only long position has credit risk.

 

2.     Credit Risk in Forwards

1.       At the settlement date, counter party entitled to receive payment faces current credit risk as the payment is due immediately and counterparty could default.

 

3.     Credit Risk in Swaps

1.       Swaps will have current credit risk on each payment exchange date and potential credit risk throughout its life.

2.       Interest rate and equity swap has maximum credit risk in the middle of the swap. Currency swap has credit risk larger between middle to the end of the swap (Since notional are exchanged).

3.       Starting of swap has zero value to both party and in the end, number of cash flows are minimal so that's the reason we have less credit risk in the beginning and end of the swaps.

4.       Credit Risk is not isolated exclusive to the swap contract written between original counterparties.  If one of counterparties defaults on some other loan and declare bankruptcy then the swap could also be impacted.

 

4.     Credit Risk in Options

1.       Only buyer faces the credit risk, they will only exercise the option when its value is positive.

2.       after the option is purchased, the credit risk is the entire value of the option and is born by the option buyer

 

5.     Managing Credit Risk

 

·         Limit Exposure.

·         Mark to Market.

·         Collateral agreements

·         netting agreements.

·         Close out netting (bankruptcy's standard).

·         Covenants (minimum credit standards).

·         Use of credit derivatives.
