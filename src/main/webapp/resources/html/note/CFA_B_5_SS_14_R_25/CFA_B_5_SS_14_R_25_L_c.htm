C) VALUE AT RISK (MARKET RISK FUNCTION)

 

1.     VaR (Value at Risk)

Probability that a return is less than a given threshold over a specific time period (Generally mentioned as 99% Weekly VaR, 95% monthly VaR).

 

Example: if a $150 million portfolio has a $5000,000 VAR at a 5% probability level for 1 month what we are saying is that over that month there is a 5% probability that the portfolio could lose more $5000,000 or there is 95% chance that losses will be less than $5000,000.

 

 

 

 

 

 

Drawbacks

a.       It doesn't provide the maximum magnitude of loss but only provides a probability of given amount of loss.

b.       VaR does not take account of the relative liquidity of different risk positions.

c.       VaR doesn't measure the counterparty credit risk, regulatory risk or operational risk.

d.       Due to various VaR calculation methodologies and subjective inputs to the model, make it difficult to compare risk with other portfolios.

e.       VaR can only be calculated for the products, which are actively traded and their historical prices are known.

 

 

A) Variance and Covariance Method (Parametric/Analytic Approach)

 

Assumptions

 

a.       Investment Returns are normally distributed and serially independent.

b.       Portfolio return distribution can be calculated using modern portfolio theory (Markowitz mean variance frontier).

c.       One day holding period is used to calculate return to measure the VaR.

 

 

VaR = Portfolio Value *[E(R) - Z-Value*

 

E (R) = Expected Return

Z Value = two tail 95% = 1.65 or 99% = 2.33

 


 

Advantages

                                       i.            VaR calculation is straight forward and easy to understand. No need of computing power.

                                     ii.            Computational simple -No need to value each asset in portfolio. Simple inputs like (variance of assets, covariance between assets and risk factors) are used in calculation.

                                   iii.            Can be applied to different time period (short or longer period as needed).,

 

Disadvantages

                                       i.            Assumes normal distribution of return.

                                     ii.            Analyst need to know the variances of every assets and the correlation between them. There variables are hard to compute for the large portfolio.

                                   iii.            Inputs are calculated based on historical return of the assets. They may not accurately predict the future.

                                   iv.            Assets with options returns are not normally distributed so their VaR cannot be calculated under this method.

 

B) Historical Method (Nonparametric Approach)

You rank historical returns from highest to lowest then pick the lowest 5% values to calculate 95% VaR. Nonparametric means asset return doesn't have to be normal.

 

 

Source: http://www.investopedia.com/articles/04/092904.asp

 

 

Advantages

                                                  i.            Easy to calculate and understand

                                                 ii.            Doesn't assume the return distribution (as opposed to normal distribution assumed by var-cov approach).

                                               iii.            It can be applied in smaller or longer periods of time.

                                               iv.            Nonparametric feature -The historical method requires minimal probability-distribution assumptions compared with other methods.

 

Disadvantages

                                                   i.            Large database of historical retunes are need for the calculation.

                                                 ii.            It assumes the distribution of return in future will be the same as past.

 

C) Monte Carlo Simulation

By using distribution of input parameters, thousands of possible outcomes are calculated based on statistical simulation. VaR is derived from these outcomes.

 

Advantages

                                       i.            Flexibility- More flexible than other methods because analyst can specify the valuation models and probability distributions of every input parameters. These parameters don’t have to base on historical data, even they can be based on analyst's future expectation.

                                     ii.            Non-parametric- It can be used to compute VaR for assets with options (derivatives).

                                   iii.            It can be used to generate more the outlier’s probabilities (disaster scenarios) than historical method would.

 

Disadvantages

                                       i.            It requires identifying key risk factors that impact the value of each individual investment held in the portfolio.

                                     ii.            It's computational intensive as it requires mathematical modelling capability.

                                   iii.            Output is as good as model inputs. (Garbage in, garbage out).

                                   iv.            Model output is dependent on the valuation models (Black Scholes or Merton etc.).
