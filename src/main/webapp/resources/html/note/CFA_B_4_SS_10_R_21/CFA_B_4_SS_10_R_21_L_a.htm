A) RELATIVE VALUE ANALYSIS

 

It is the process of comparing credit bond market to determine which sectors, structure or issuer will outperform others based on different characteristics.

 

The main methodologies for credit relative-value maximization are:

 

·         Total return analysis;

Analysis of business cycle, macro-economic conditions, and credit fundamentals of credit issuers. Total return patterns are necessary to optimize portfolio

Performance.

 

·         primary market analysis;

Focuses on spread analysis relative to supply and demand of credit issues.

1.      Cyclical Changes

Counterintuitive, increase number of new corporate issues causes narrowing the spread because demand also increases. Generally, increase in supply should cause spread to go bigger.

 

2.      Secular changes

Intermediate term corporate bonds with bullet maturity are becoming more common than bonds with options (callable, putable). Securities with options may trade at premium due to their scarcity. People seeking longer term maturity should pay premium due to scarcity.

 

 

·         Liquidity and Trading analysis;

Analysis of the liquidity among sectors, issuer and structures. Due to advancement of trading technology, large number of investors are willing to give up additional return by investing in issues which are more liquid.

 

 

·         Secondary trading rationales and constraints analysis;

Following are the trade types which are done on the secondary market:

 

1. Yield/Spread Pickup Trades

Generally, investors buy lower quality bond and sell higher quality bond to earn more yield.  Analysis should be done at total return basis (not just higher yield basis).

2. Credit Upside Trades

Buy bonds, which manager expects to upgrade in rating.

3. Credit-Defense

Trades-Sell bonds, which manager expects to downgrade in rating.

4. New Issue Swaps

Since on the run treasury securities are more liquid so many portfolio managers tend to invest in new issues.

5.Sector-Rotation Trades

Shifts the portfolio to a sector or industry which will outperform on a total return basis.

6.Curve-Adjustment Trades

These trades are taken to alter the portfolio’s duration to be consistent with expected yield curve changes.

7.Structure Trades

Convert into structures (e.g., callable structures, bullet structures, and puttable structures) that are expected to have better performance given expected yield curve changes.

8. Cash Flow Reinvestment

For Cash flow reinvestment purpose, investors trade into the secondary market.

 

 

·        Spread analysis(OAS, Nominal, Z-spread, swap spread)

 

When spreads are projected to widen that means bond's value will go down and when the spreads are projected to narrow that means bond's price will go up. Generally longer duration bonds will have more impact of duration than shorter duration bond.

 

1.      Mean reversion analysis

It's assumed that spread will return to their historical mean.

If the Bond's spread is significantly greater than the historical mean, then buy the bond because spread will revert to its mean in future(low).

If the Bond's spread is significantly less than the historical mean, then sell the bond because spread will revert to its mean in future(high).

 

2.      Quality Spread analysis

Buy good quality, wider spread bond because investor think, their spread will get narrow in future.

 

3.      Percentage yield analysis

Like mean reversion analysis, calculate a ratio of (Corporate bond Yield/Treasury bond yield) of same duration. if ratio is higher than historical ration then yield is expected to fall so buy the corporate bond. If opposite is true, then sell the bond.

 

·         Structure analysis (Callable, puttable, sinking funds)

 

1.      Bullet Bond

Short Term (1-5 years)-Usually used on the front end of the barbell strategy, long term treasuries are used at the long end.

Medium Term (5-12 years)-Most popular for high yield market.

Long Term (30 years)- Offer positive convexity but at a cost of increased duration.

 

2.      Callable Bond

 

Value of call = Value of non-callable bond - Value of callable bond.

When interest rates fall, price of callable bond doesn't increase as much as non-callable bond due to negative convexity (As shown below). So they underperform.

When interest rates rise, price of callable bond doesn't fall as much as non-callable bond (possibility of being called) so they outperform.

 

 

 

3.      Putable Bond

Value of put = Puttable bond value - option free bond.

 

The price of a puttable bond will behave same way as an option-free bond at low yield levels. As rates rise, the puttable bond's price will decrease at the same rate as an option-free bond, but the decline will be lessened because of the value of the put option.

Generally, investors invest in puttable bonds when they expect interest rate to rise.  Models that exist to calculate the value of putable bonds usually ignore the risk that the issuer will default so it's difficult to find out correct valuation.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

4.      Sinking Funds

Generally, they are priced at a discount to par. Issuer keeps retiring some portion of an issue on periodic basis and replace with lower yield bonds in case of declining interest rate environment. It's also beneficial from investors perspective because early payment of debt reduces the default risk as well. Due to these features, price of sinking fund bonds doesn't fall as much as callable bonds, when interest rate rises.

 

 

 

·         Credit analysis

Generally, involves analysis of 4 Cs (Character, capacity, capital, and collateral).

 

·         Capacity to pay (Cash flow analysis of corporates)

·         Collateral quality (Servicer analysis in case of MBS)

·         Ability to collect (especially for municipal taxes)

·         Ability/willingness to pay (For Sovereigns)
