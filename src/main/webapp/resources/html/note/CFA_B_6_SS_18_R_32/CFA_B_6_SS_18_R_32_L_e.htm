2.     Real estate:

a.      Closed end fund: SI-IRR must be reported using at least quarterly rate of return.

b.      Composites must be defined by grouping accounts with similar objective, strategy, and vintage year.

c.       External valuation done at least once a year

d.      Real estate investment must be valued at least quarterly. 

e.      Total Return combines both IR (Income return) and CR (Capital return).

 

3.     Valuation hierarchy (Proxy for fair value of assets)

Order in which asset value should be picked.  Next level valuation should only be used if previous level of valuation is not available. (If market price of an asset is not available then use dealer's quote for valuing the asset)

 

a.       Market Price (Active quoted price by dealer of the same security)

b.       Quoted prices for identical or similar investments in markets that are not active. (Dealer's quote)

c.       Market-based inputs other than quoted prices that are observable for the investment. (Example P/E, P/S)

d.       Subjective, unobservable inputs. (discounting cash flows).

 

5.     Performance Valuation Vs Verification under GIPS

 

1.       In Verification, 3rd party verifier picks few sample composites (based on portfolios)

2.       Performance evaluation is done at the composite level where as verification is done at the firm level.

3.       Performance evaluation is done after the verification is done and PE is stricter then verification.

4.       Performance evaluation report cannot be issued unless verification report has issued.

 

6.     After-Tax Return Calculation Challenges

 

1.       The GIPS standards do not require compliant firms to present after-tax returns for composites.

2.       ETFs and MF can have used as a proxy of an after-tax benchmark.

3.       Paper portfolio can also be created to mimic client specific after tax return.

4.       Two methods for incorporating the effects on after tax return

a. Pre-liquidation method: This method ignores tax effect on unrealized gains and losses (overstating after tax return).

b. Mark to liquidation method: this method also considers taxes on unrealized gains and losses but ignores the value of tax deferrals (overstating tax liability and understating after tax return).

 

Points

 

1.       If a composite includes non-fee-paying portfolios, the firm must present the percentage of composite assets represented by non-fee-paying portfolios as of each annual period end.

2.       A composite includes portfolios with bundled fees, the firm must present the percentage of composite assets represented by portfolios with bundled fees as of each annual period end.

3.       Generally, all of firm's composites do need to have the same beginning and ending annual valuation dates (except in few cases).

4.       Composite should be valued monthly (or at large cash flow), present the performance quarterly to the client quarterly with annualized rate of return, dispersion in rate of return based on the annual period, benchmark performance annual return.

5.       Quarterly performance reporting with 30 days after the reporting period being recommended.

6.       Trade date is when the transaction takes place, whereas settlement date is when the exchange of cash, securities, and paperwork involved in a transaction is completed.

7.       The GIPS standards require cash and cash equivalents to be included in total return calculations for all asset classes.
