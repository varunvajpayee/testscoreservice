A) CAPITAL MARKET EXPECTATIONS

Capital Market expectations are macro expectations, which are about asset classes whereas micro expectations are about individual securities.

1)  Capital Market Expectations – 7 steps

A.      Determine the specific capital market expectation needed.

B.      Reference to the historical data.

C.      Specify the model.

D.      Collect the best data possible.

E.       Consider the current investment environment by using experience and judgement.

F.       Formulate set of expectations.

G.      Monitor performance and refine the process.

2.     Problem in forecasting

1. Limitations of economic data:

       A.   Time lag – outdated data is less useful.

       B.   Revisions to the original values are common.

       C.   Data definition and calculation methodology may change.

       D.  Change of rebase.

 

2. Data Measurement Errors:

      A.   Transcription errors – e.g. keying in errors.

      B.   Survivorship bias – a data series include only those who have survived. e.g. a share index. Overestimate returns and underestimate risks.

      C.   Appraised data – based on estimate due to lack of liquidity and trades.  Results in 1) lower correlation 2) lower standard deviation.

 

3. Use of long time historical data

 

Advantages:

1) longer time period is statistically required.

2) more precise

3) less sensitive to the starting and ending point.

 

Disadvantages:

1) Regime change – nonstationary

2) Relevant time period can be short

3) can use outdated data when extending data to the distant past.   

 

** asynchronism – high frequency data (e.g. daily, weekly instead of monthly/quarterly) can distort the correlation by making it seem less correlated with other classes.   

 

**Ex post risk can be a biased measure of ax ante risk – overestimate returns & underestimate risks.

 

4. Biases in analysts’ methods

 A. Data mining bias – look for data until analyst finds some statistical pattern.  But it is not likely to persist.

 B. Time period bias – a certain data set is only applicable to a certain time.

5. Psychological traps: 

                                       i.            Anchoring trap – putting too much weight on the first set of information received

                                     ii.            Status quo trap – reluctant to change the most current data

                                   iii.            Confirmation bias – looking for information that supports his/her views

                                   iv.            Overconfidence trap – 1) Ignore past mistakes 2) believe most people share same view 3) too narrow a range of possibilities

                                     v.            Recallability trap – overly influenced by data that have left a strong impression on a person’s memory.   Tend to use data that easily comes to mind 

                                   vi.            Prudence trap – temper forecast so that they do not appear extreme.  Being too cautious

 

 

3) Forecasting Tools

 

 1. Statistical tools

·         Sample Estimators:

A.   Arithmetic mean – best represents in a single period.

B.   Geometric mean return – best represent multiperiod growth (account for compounding effect).

The arithmetic mean return is always higher than the geometric mean return

 

·         Shrinkage estimators:  weighted average of a historical estimate + analyst’s belief in the estimate. It reduces the impact of extreme values in historical estimates.

 

·         Time series methods – used to estimate near term volatility (volatility clustering).

3.      Discounted cash flow models

(DCF models) express the idea that an asset’s value is the present value of its (expected) cash flows.

 

Gordon Growth Model

E(Re) = D1/P + g

 

E(Re) = Expected return of this year (Year 0)

D1= Expected dividend of next year (Year 1)

P = This year's share price (Year 0)

g = real growth in earnings

 

Grinold-Kroner model

 

Expected Return = Expected Income Return + Expected Nominal Earnings Growth + Expected reprising return 

E(Re) = D1/P -∆S + i +g + ∆P/E

 

∆S = # of shares outstanding (-∆S = Repurchase yield).

i = inflation

∆P/E = change in PE ratio

 

 

a.       Expected Income Return: D/P - ∆S

b.       Expected Nominal Earnings Growth: i+g

c.       Expected reprising return: ∆P/E

 

 In addition to Gordon Growth Model inputs, Grinold & Knower model also consider share repurchase, change in P/E ratio, and clearly distinguish between nominal and real earnings growth.

 

4.      The Risk Premium Approach (Build Up Approach)

You begin with risk free rate and you keep adding other risk premium to compensate the exposure of investor's risk assets(equity).

 

E(Rb) = Real risk free rate + Inflation premium + Default risk premium + Illiquid risk premium + Maturity premium + Tax premium

 

E(Rb) = The expected bond return

 

Note that maturity premium represents a difference in spread on 1 year interest maturity vs 10-year maturity.

Expected equity return E(Re) = YTD on a long-term government bond + Equity risk premium

6) Survey, Panel, and Judgement

Asking a group of experts for their expectations and forming capital market expectation based on that. In the panel, you continue poll same group over time. In judgement, analyst uses his/her judgment to form capital market expectation, often methods like checklists are used.
