A) HUMAN CAPITAL

 

1.     Human vs Financial capital

 

1.       Financial Capital: The value of the investor’s investments in tradable assets.

2.       Human Capital: The present value of an investor’s expected future labor income

 Normally, younger investors tend to have more human capital than financial capital.

 

 

 

 

Stages of wealth accumulation

 

·         Foundation (Minimum Financial Capital and Maximum Human Capital)- Early Education phase.

·         Accumulation (Increasing Financial Capital and Decreasing Human Capital) Mid-Career.

·         Maintenance (Maximum Financial Capital and Minimum Human Capital) Retirement.

·         Distribution at Death, bequests.

 

 

2.     Financial asset allocation related to human capital:

The greater the proportion of investment that should be in stock

 

1.       Younger the investor

2.       Safer the labor income of an investor

3.       Lower the correlation of an investor’s labor income with the stock market

4.       Greater the flexibility of an investor to adjust their labor income.

 

3.     Life Insurance related to human capital (Mortality Risk)

Life insurance and life annuities can protect against human capital uncertainty (Mortality risk). The general rule is that more life insurance is needed as human capital gets higher.

1.      Greater life insurance

§ More risk averse an investor (the more conservative the financial asset allocation)

§ Greater the desire for a bequest at death

§ Greater an investor’s salary, the larger the investor’s implied human capital

 

2.      Less life insurance

§ Older an investor, the less the investor’s human capital

§ Greater an investor’s wealth

§ Greater the correction between an investor’s human capital and financial capital

4.      Earning Risk

Risk that your income experiences a disruption.

Bond like Human Capital is less risky in nature. Income is stable, predictable, and uncorrelated with the stock market. Example clerk/teacher with stable job.

Equity like human capital is more risk in nature. It's more correlated with stock market (where compensation is tied to performance bonus).  For example, trader who pay is related to his stock's performance.

5.     Financial market risk

The risk that adverse volatility in financial markets during retirement will significantly reduce the value of the investor’s retirement portfolio assets

 

·         Higher allocation to stock generally increases the financial risk for an investor in retirement.

·         Higher allocation to fixed income generally decrease the financial risk (because fixed income investments normally have less volatility)

 

6.     Longevity risk

The risk that the investor will outlive the assets in their investment portfolio

 

·         Higher allocation to stock generally reduces the longevity risk (because stocks normally have higher expected returns)

·         Higher allocation to fixed income generally increase the longevity risk (lower expected returns)

7.     Annuities

It's a perpetual stream of cash-flows which you receive by paying for a large amount upfront.  They are used to mitigate the longevity risk.

1.       Fixed Rate Annuities -  Provides same amount each period and doesn't get adjusted for inflation. Payments are determined based on locked in interest rate at the time purchase.

2.       Variable Rate Annuities - Adjust cash flows based on the performance of the underlying asset's portfolio. Since it's based on assets portfolio so it protects against inflation. However, there is also a risk associated with the performance of underlying asset's portfolio.
